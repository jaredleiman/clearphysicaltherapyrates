Does Medicare Cover Physical Therapy? Coverage, Costs, and Limits

By ClearPhysicalTherapyRates · April 2026 · 8 min read

Yes — Medicare Part B covers outpatient physical therapy when it is medically necessary and ordered by a physician or qualifying provider. Medicare pays 80% of the approved amount, and the patient pays the remaining 20% coinsurance after meeting the annual Part B deductible ($257 in 2026).

What Medicare Covers

Medicare Part B covers outpatient physical therapy services including evaluation and re-evaluation (CPT 97161-97164), therapeutic exercise (97110), manual therapy (97140), therapeutic activities (97530), neuromuscular re-education (97112), gait training (97116), and therapeutic modalities such as ultrasound and electrical stimulation. Services must be provided by a licensed physical therapist and must be medically necessary — meaning the treatment is designed to restore, improve, or maintain function that has been impaired by illness, injury, or medical condition.

The Therapy Spending Threshold

Medicare uses a spending threshold (previously called the "therapy cap") that triggers additional scrutiny when a patient's therapy spending reaches a certain level. For 2026, the combined threshold for physical therapy and speech-language pathology is approximately $2,330. Occupational therapy has its own separate threshold of approximately $2,330.

The threshold is not a hard cap — it does not stop coverage. When spending exceeds the threshold, the therapist must add a KX modifier to claims certifying that services are medically necessary. Claims above the threshold may also be subject to targeted medical review — meaning Medicare may audit the documentation to verify medical necessity.

What the KX modifier means: By adding the KX modifier to a claim, the therapist is certifying that the service is medically necessary and that documentation in the medical record supports this. If an audit finds that the documentation doesn't support medical necessity, the therapist may be required to repay the claims. The KX modifier is a compliance commitment, not just a billing checkbox.

How Much Does Medicare PT Cost the Patient?

After the annual Part B deductible is met, patients pay 20% coinsurance on all PT services. For a typical session billed at $150-$200 (Medicare allowed amount), the patient's share is $30-$40 per session. Patients with Medigap supplemental insurance may have their 20% coinsurance covered, resulting in zero out-of-pocket cost. Medicare Advantage patients pay according to their specific plan's cost-sharing rules, which may include flat copays instead of coinsurance.

Medicare PT in Different Settings

Medicare covers PT in several settings with different payment rules. Outpatient clinics and private practices bill under the Medicare Physician Fee Schedule. Hospital outpatient departments bill under the Outpatient Prospective Payment System and may charge facility fees. Home health PT is covered under the home health benefit with no patient cost-sharing. Skilled nursing facility PT is covered under Part A during a qualifying SNF stay.

No Referral Required

Medicare does not require a physician referral for outpatient physical therapy. Patients can go directly to a PT under Medicare — though the therapist must obtain a physician's certification of the plan of care within 30 days of treatment initiation. In practice, most Medicare PT episodes begin with a physician order, but the referral is not a coverage requirement.

When Medicare Stops Covering PT

Medicare stops covering PT when the patient has met their functional goals and no longer needs skilled therapy, the patient is no longer making progress and maintenance can be performed independently, or the documentation does not support continued medical necessity. However, under the Jimmo v. Sebelius settlement, Medicare cannot deny coverage solely because a patient isn't improving. Skilled therapy to maintain function or prevent decline in patients with progressive conditions remains covered as long as the service requires the skills of a therapist.

The Bottom Line

Medicare provides robust coverage for physical therapy — no referral required, no hard visit limit, and relatively low patient cost-sharing. The key consideration for patients approaching the spending threshold is ensuring their therapist documents medical necessity thoroughly. For most Medicare beneficiaries, PT is one of the most accessible and affordable rehabilitation benefits available.
