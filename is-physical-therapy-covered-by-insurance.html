Is Physical Therapy Covered by Insurance? What You Need to Know

By ClearPhysicalTherapyRates · April 2026 · 8 min read

Yes — the vast majority of health insurance plans in the United States cover physical therapy. Under the Affordable Care Act, rehabilitative and habilitative services (which include physical therapy) are classified as essential health benefits that most plans must cover. However, the specifics of coverage — visit limits, copays, referral requirements, and prior authorization — vary significantly between plan types.

Coverage by Plan Type

Employer-Sponsored Plans (PPO/HMO)

Most employer plans cover physical therapy with a copay of $20-$50 per visit for in-network providers. Visit limits vary — some plans allow 20-30 visits per year, others are unlimited as long as treatment is medically necessary. HMO plans typically require a referral from your primary care physician; PPO plans usually do not. Some plans require prior authorization after a set number of visits (commonly 12-20).

ACA Marketplace Plans

All ACA marketplace plans must cover rehabilitative services including physical therapy as an essential health benefit. Coverage details vary by metal tier — Bronze plans have higher cost-sharing, Gold/Platinum plans have lower out-of-pocket costs. Visit limits and authorization requirements vary by specific plan.

Medicare

Medicare Part B covers outpatient physical therapy at 80% of the approved amount after the Part B deductible. There is no hard visit limit, but a spending threshold applies — once your combined PT and speech therapy spending exceeds approximately $2,330 in a year, claims require additional justification and may be reviewed. Medicare does not require a referral for PT.

Medicaid

Medicaid covers physical therapy in all states, though specific rules vary. Coverage may require prior authorization, may limit the number of visits per year, and reimbursement rates to providers are typically lower than commercial insurance. Children on Medicaid have broader coverage through EPSDT.

Workers' Compensation

Workers' comp covers physical therapy for work-related injuries, usually without visit limits as long as treatment is related to the work injury and authorized by the workers' comp insurer. Workers' comp has its own fee schedules and documentation requirements separate from health insurance.

Direct Access: Do You Need a Referral?

As of 2026, all 50 states and DC allow some form of direct access to physical therapy — meaning you can see a PT without a physician referral. However, direct access rules vary by state. Some states have unrestricted direct access (no referral needed, no limitations), while others allow direct access with limitations (e.g., the PT must refer to a physician after a set number of visits if the patient doesn't improve, or the PT must have a minimum number of years of experience).

Important: even in states with direct access, your insurance plan may still require a referral for coverage. The state law allows you to see a PT without a referral, but your insurer can require a referral as a coverage condition. Always verify with your specific plan.

Money-saving tip: Direct access eliminates the cost of a doctor's visit just to get a referral. In states with unrestricted direct access and plans that don't require referrals, going straight to PT can save you $50-$200 in physician visit costs.

Common Coverage Limitations

  • Visit limits — Many plans cap PT visits at 20-60 per calendar year. Once you hit the limit, you pay full price for additional visits.
  • Prior authorization — Some plans require pre-approval before PT begins or after a certain number of visits. Failing to obtain authorization can result in denied claims.
  • In-network requirements — Out-of-network PT visits typically cost significantly more due to higher coinsurance rates and possible balance billing.
  • Medical necessity — Payers can deny continued PT if they determine the treatment is no longer medically necessary. Strong documentation of ongoing functional improvement is essential.
  • Maintenance therapy exclusions — Most plans don't cover PT that's solely for maintaining current function without expected improvement (similar to chiropractic maintenance therapy rules).

How to Verify Your Coverage

Before starting physical therapy, call the member services number on your insurance card and ask these specific questions: Is physical therapy a covered benefit on my plan? Do I need a referral or prior authorization? Is there an annual visit limit — and if so, how many visits have I used this year? What is my copay or coinsurance for in-network outpatient PT? Has my deductible been met? Is the PT practice I want to go to in-network? Most PT offices will also verify your benefits for you if you provide your insurance information before the first visit.

The Bottom Line

Physical therapy is covered by virtually all health insurance plans in the US, thanks to the ACA's essential health benefits requirement. The key variables are your specific copay/coinsurance, any visit limits, and whether you need a referral or prior authorization. Verify before you go, use in-network providers, and take advantage of direct access in your state to avoid unnecessary physician visits.
