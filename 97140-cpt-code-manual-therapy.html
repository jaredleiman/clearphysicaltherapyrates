CPT Code 97140: Manual Therapy Billing Guide

By ClearPhysicalTherapyRates · April 2026 · 8 min read

CPT 97140 — Manual Therapy Techniques — covers skilled hands-on treatment performed by the therapist. It is one of the highest-value codes in physical therapy because it represents direct, skilled intervention that only a trained therapist can provide. It is also one of the most scrutinized codes by payers because of its potential for overuse.

What 97140 Covers

97140 is defined as manual therapy techniques including mobilization and manipulation. Each unit represents 15 minutes of direct one-on-one hands-on time. Covered techniques include joint mobilization (grades I-IV oscillatory techniques to improve joint mobility), joint manipulation (high-velocity low-amplitude thrust techniques), soft tissue mobilization (myofascial release, trigger point therapy, cross-friction massage, instrument-assisted soft tissue mobilization), manual lymphatic drainage, manual traction, and passive range of motion when performed as a skilled manual technique with clinical assessment.

97140 vs. 97110: Hands-On vs. Exercise

The fundamental distinction is who is doing the work. In 97140, the therapist is performing hands-on techniques on the patient. In 97110, the patient is performing exercises with therapist guidance. A therapist mobilizing a patient's glenohumeral joint is 97140. A patient performing shoulder flexion exercises with therapist cueing is 97110.

97140 vs. Massage (97124)

97124 (massage) is a separate code for therapeutic massage. The distinction between soft tissue mobilization (billed under 97140) and massage (97124) is that 97140 soft tissue work is technique-specific and targeted — the therapist is applying specific pressure to identified restrictions, trigger points, or fascial adhesions based on evaluation findings. Massage (97124) is more general tissue manipulation. Many payers do not cover 97124, making this distinction practically important.

Payer watch: Some payers — particularly certain Medicare Advantage plans and Medicaid programs — have begun limiting the number of 97140 units that can be billed per session or per episode. Check your top payers' specific policies on manual therapy unit limits to avoid unexpected denials.

Documentation Requirements

97140 documentation must demonstrate the skilled nature of the hands-on intervention. Each note should include the specific technique performed (not just "manual therapy"), the body region and specific structures treated, the grade or intensity of mobilization (for joint mobilization, document the grade and direction), the clinical reasoning for the technique (what restriction or dysfunction is being addressed), the patient's response during and after the technique, measurable outcomes (pre- and post-treatment ROM, pain level change), and exact time spent on manual therapy.

Strong documentation: "Performed Grade III PA mobilization to L4-L5 segment — 3 sets of 30 seconds each. Pre-treatment lumbar extension 15°, post-treatment 25°. Followed with iliopsoas myofascial release, 5 minutes bilateral. Patient reported pain decreased from 6/10 to 3/10 post-intervention. Total manual therapy time: 16 minutes."

Billing Multiple Manual Therapy Units

Billing 3+ units of 97140 in a single session (45+ minutes of hands-on work) is a common audit trigger. While it is clinically appropriate for some patients, the documentation must justify the extended manual therapy time. Payers question whether 45+ minutes of manual therapy in one session is reasonable and whether the patient wouldn't be better served by a balance of manual therapy and active interventions (exercise, functional activities).

Best practice: if you're regularly billing 3+ units of 97140 per session, review whether some of that time would be more accurately coded as another service, and ensure your documentation explains the clinical rationale for extended manual therapy.

Common Billing Errors

  • Billing 97140 for passive modalities — Hot packs, ultrasound, and electrical stimulation are not manual therapy. They have their own codes (97010, 97035, 97032).
  • Billing 97140 for stretching performed by the patient — If the patient is actively stretching, that's 97110. If the therapist is performing passive stretching with hands-on technique and clinical assessment, that can be 97140.
  • Over-billing 97140 units — Consistently high 97140 utilization relative to peers triggers audit review. A balanced treatment session typically includes 1-2 units of manual therapy combined with exercise and functional training.
  • Vague documentation — "Manual therapy to lumbar spine, 15 minutes" will be denied on audit. Specify the technique, the target structure, and the measurable outcome.

The Bottom Line

97140 represents the highest-skill intervention in your treatment toolkit — the hands-on work that only a trained therapist can provide. Document it with the specificity it deserves: named techniques, targeted structures, measurable outcomes, and clear clinical reasoning. Payers are willing to pay for skilled manual therapy when the documentation proves the skill.
