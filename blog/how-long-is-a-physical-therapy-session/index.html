How Long Is a Physical Therapy Session? What to Expect

By ClearPhysicalTherapyRates · April 2026 · 6 min read

A typical physical therapy session lasts 30 to 60 minutes, with most sessions falling in the 45-minute range. Your first visit (the initial evaluation) usually runs longer — 45 to 90 minutes — because it includes a comprehensive assessment in addition to treatment.

Session Length by Visit Type

Initial Evaluation (First Visit)

Your first PT visit is the longest, typically 45 to 90 minutes. During this visit, the therapist takes your health history, reviews imaging and medical records, performs a physical examination (strength, range of motion, flexibility, balance, movement quality), identifies your functional limitations, develops a treatment plan with specific goals, and often begins some initial treatment. The evaluation is a critical visit — it establishes the baseline measurements that track your progress throughout treatment.

Standard Follow-Up Session

Follow-up treatment sessions typically last 30 to 60 minutes. A standard session might include a brief check-in on symptoms and progress (5 minutes), hands-on manual therapy to address joint or tissue restrictions (10-15 minutes), therapeutic exercises targeting your specific deficits (15-20 minutes), and functional training or activity practice (5-10 minutes). Some practices schedule 30-minute sessions for patients with straightforward conditions needing only exercise instruction and progression, while others schedule 60-minute sessions for patients requiring more complex, multi-component treatment.

Re-Evaluation

Periodic re-evaluations (every 10 visits or every 30 days for Medicare patients) take 30 to 45 minutes. These visits include re-testing baseline measurements to document progress, updating the treatment plan, and usually some treatment time.

What Affects Session Length

Several factors influence how long your PT sessions last. Your condition's complexity determines whether you need a 30-minute focused session or a 60-minute multi-component treatment. Your insurance plan may influence session length — some plans authorize a specific number of units per visit, which directly determines the treatment time. The practice model matters too — mill-style clinics that see multiple patients simultaneously tend to have shorter individual contact time, while one-on-one practices spend the full session with you. Your stage of recovery also plays a role — early sessions may be longer (more hands-on work, more education) while later sessions may be shorter (primarily exercise progression and monitoring).

Quality indicator: During your session, the therapist should be directly working with you for the majority of the scheduled time. If you're left alone doing exercises on machines for most of the visit while the therapist treats other patients, you may want to ask about the practice's patient-to-therapist ratio and consider whether a one-on-one practice would better serve your needs.

Session Length vs. Billing

It's important to understand that your session length and your bill are not the same thing. Physical therapy is billed by the number of services provided (coded in 15-minute units), not by total time in the clinic. You might be in the clinic for 60 minutes but only receive 45 minutes of billable treatment — the remaining time is check-in, setup, transitions between activities, and home exercise instruction that may not be billed separately.

Conversely, a focused 30-minute session with 3 billable units of treatment could cost the same as a 45-minute session with 3 units if the same codes are billed.

How Many Sessions Per Week?

Most treatment plans call for 2-3 sessions per week during the initial phase of treatment, tapering to 1-2 sessions per week as you progress, and eventually transitioning to an independent home exercise program. Some conditions — particularly post-surgical rehabilitation — may start at 3 sessions per week, while chronic conditions may begin at 1-2 sessions per week from the start.

The Bottom Line

Most PT sessions last 30-60 minutes, with your first visit running longer due to the evaluation component. The quality of care during that time matters more than the duration — a focused 30-minute one-on-one session often produces better outcomes than a 60-minute session where you're sharing the therapist's attention with other patients. Ask your therapist about their treatment model so you know what to expect.
