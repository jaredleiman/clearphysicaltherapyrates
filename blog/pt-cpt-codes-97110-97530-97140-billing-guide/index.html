CPT Codes 97110, 97530, and 97140: The Complete PT Billing Guide for 2026

April 202610 min readClearPhysicalTherapyRates.com

CPT codes 97110, 97530, and 97140 account for the majority of physical therapy Medicare billing. Getting these codes right — correct code selection, complete documentation, proper bundling — is the foundation of PT billing compliance. Here's everything that matters for 2026.

CPT 97110 — Therapeutic Exercise

Official AMA Definition

97110: Therapeutic procedure, 1 or more areas, each 15 minutes; therapeutic exercises to develop strength and endurance, range of motion and flexibility.

2026 Medicare Non-Facility Rate: $31.87 per 15-minute unit

97110 is the most commonly billed PT code — and one of the most frequently audited. The code covers any exercise performed by the patient under the skilled supervision of the therapist, where the exercise is designed to develop strength, endurance, range of motion, or flexibility.

The key documentation requirement is establishing skilled care: the therapist's involvement must be more than oversight. Documentation should reflect that the therapist is actively modifying the exercise program based on the patient's response, not simply counting repetitions.

Documentation Requirements for 97110

  • Specific exercise(s) performed, not just "therapeutic exercise"
  • Sets, repetitions, resistance, or other measurable parameters
  • Patient response and any modifications made during the session
  • Relationship to the functional goal being addressed
  • Skilled justification — why this requires a licensed PT rather than a home exercise program

CPT 97530 — Therapeutic Activities

Official AMA Definition

97530: Therapeutic activities, direct (one-on-one) patient contact by the provider (use of dynamic activities to improve functional performance), each 15 minutes.

2026 Medicare Non-Facility Rate: $32.21 per 15-minute unit

97530 covers functional activities — task-specific training designed to improve the patient's ability to perform real-world activities. The distinction from 97110 is that 97530 involves dynamic, functional movement rather than isolated strengthening or range-of-motion exercises.

Examples of 97530 activities: balance training with functional tasks, gait training with obstacles, transfer training, stair negotiation, simulated work activities, and functional reaching or carrying tasks.

Common Billing Error

97110 and 97530 are frequently billed together on the same date of service. This is appropriate when the services are genuinely distinct — for example, isolated strengthening (97110) followed by functional gait training (97530). However, billing both codes for activities that are essentially the same service documented differently creates audit exposure. Each code billed must be supported by documentation that clearly distinguishes the service from the other.

CPT 97140 — Manual Therapy

Official AMA Definition

97140: Manual therapy techniques (e.g., mobilization/manipulation, manual lymphatic drainage, manual traction), 1 or more regions, each 15 minutes.

2026 Medicare Non-Facility Rate: $29.03 per 15-minute unit

97140 covers hands-on manual techniques applied by the therapist. The code is appropriate for joint mobilization, soft tissue mobilization, manual traction, myofascial release, and similar hands-on interventions.

Critical distinction: 97140 requires direct, skilled application of manual techniques by the therapist. It cannot be billed for supervising a patient performing self-mobilization or for mechanical traction (which is coded as 97012).

Bundling Rules: What You Can and Cannot Bill Together

NCCI (National Correct Coding Initiative) edits govern which CPT codes can be billed together. For PT codes, several common combinations have changed or require attention in 2026:

  • 97110 + 97530: Can be billed together with appropriate documentation distinguishing the services. No NCCI edit prevents this combination.
  • 97110 + 97140: Can be billed together. Document clearly which time was spent on exercise versus manual therapy.
  • 97530 + 97140: Can be billed together. Same documentation principles apply.
  • 97110/97530/97140 + 97012: Mechanical traction can be billed with therapeutic exercise or activities when performed as separate, distinct services in the same session.
  • Multiple units of the same code: Each additional 15-minute unit of the same code must represent a distinct, separately documented service period.

The 8-Minute Rule for Time-Based Codes

97110, 97530, and 97140 are all time-based codes billed in 15-minute units. The 8-minute rule governs when you can bill for a unit of a time-based code:

  • A single 15-minute unit requires at least 8 minutes of direct service
  • Two units require at least 23 minutes (8 minutes for the second unit, on top of a full 15 for the first)
  • Three units require at least 38 minutes

Your documentation must reflect the total timed service minutes to support the number of units billed. "97110 x 2 units, 97140 x 1 unit" requires documentation of at least 38 total minutes of direct timed service.

What Changed in 2026

Beyond the conversion factor reduction affecting all three codes, 2026 brought updated guidance on documentation specificity for therapeutic exercises billed to Medicare. CMS clarified in sub-regulatory guidance that documentation of "therapeutic exercise" without specificity about the exercise performed, the parameters, and the skilled justification is insufficient to support 97110. This guidance formalizes what auditors have been applying in practice but now explicitly states the expectation.
