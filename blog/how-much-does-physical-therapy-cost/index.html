How Much Does Physical Therapy Cost? With and Without Insurance

By ClearPhysicalTherapyRates · April 2026 · 9 min read

Physical therapy costs vary widely depending on whether you have insurance, where you live, and what type of treatment you receive. Without insurance, expect to pay $50 to $350 per session. With insurance, your out-of-pocket cost typically drops to $20 to $75 per visit in copays or coinsurance.

Cost Without Insurance

Initial Evaluation

The first physical therapy visit includes a comprehensive evaluation — health history, movement assessment, strength and flexibility testing, and development of a treatment plan. Initial evaluations typically cost $150 to $350 without insurance, depending on the complexity and the practice's rates.

Follow-Up Treatment Sessions

Standard treatment sessions (45-60 minutes) typically cost $75 to $200 without insurance. The cost depends on which services are provided — a session with manual therapy, therapeutic exercise, and modalities costs more than a session with exercise alone because more billing codes are involved.

Specialized Services

Certain specialized PT services carry higher costs. Pelvic floor physical therapy, vestibular rehabilitation, and dry needling sessions may range from $100 to $300 per visit due to the additional training and time required.

Cost With Insurance

With insurance coverage, your cost depends on your plan's cost-sharing structure:

  • Copay plans — You pay a flat copay per visit, typically $20 to $50 for in-network PT. This is the same regardless of what services are provided during the session.
  • Coinsurance plans — You pay a percentage of the allowed amount, typically 10-30%. For a session with an allowed amount of $200, a 20% coinsurance means you pay $40.
  • High-deductible plans — You pay 100% of the negotiated rate until your deductible is met. For a $2,500 deductible, you could pay full price for the first 10-15 sessions before insurance kicks in.

The deductible trap: Many patients with high-deductible health plans are surprised to receive bills for the full negotiated rate — sometimes $150-$250 per session — because they haven't met their deductible. Ask your PT office to estimate your per-session cost based on your specific deductible status before starting treatment.

What Drives the Cost of Each Session

Physical therapy sessions are billed by code, not by time. A 45-minute session that includes three billed services costs more than a 45-minute session with two services. The most common codes and their approximate costs are:

  • Evaluation (97161-97163) — $100-$250 depending on complexity level
  • Therapeutic Exercise, 97110 — $30-$60 per unit (15 min)
  • Manual Therapy, 97140 — $35-$70 per unit
  • Therapeutic Activities, 97530 — $30-$60 per unit
  • Neuromuscular Re-education, 97112 — $35-$65 per unit
  • Modalities (ultrasound, e-stim) — $15-$40 per application

A typical session might include 2 units of 97110, 1 unit of 97140, and 1 unit of 97530 — totaling 4 billable units across three codes. The combined charge could be $150-$250 before insurance.

How Many Sessions Will You Need?

The total cost of physical therapy depends heavily on how many sessions your condition requires:

  • Acute injury (ankle sprain, minor strain) — 4-8 sessions over 2-4 weeks
  • Post-surgical rehabilitation (ACL, rotator cuff, joint replacement) — 12-24 sessions over 2-4 months
  • Chronic pain management — 8-16 sessions over 2-3 months, possibly with periodic maintenance
  • Complex conditions (stroke, neurological) — 20-40+ sessions over several months

Ways to Reduce Your Cost

  • Ask about cash-pay rates — Many PT practices offer discounted rates for patients who pay at the time of service without filing insurance. Cash rates can be 20-40% lower than insurance-billed rates.
  • Use in-network providers — In-network PT costs significantly less than out-of-network due to negotiated rate discounts.
  • Use your HSA/FSA — Physical therapy is an eligible expense, saving you 20-35% through pre-tax dollars.
  • Ask about visit packages — Some practices offer prepaid packages at reduced per-visit rates.
  • Consider direct access — Most states allow you to see a PT without a physician referral, saving you the cost of a doctor's visit just to get a referral.

Medicare Physical Therapy Costs

Medicare Part B covers outpatient physical therapy at 80% of the allowed amount after the annual deductible ($257 in 2026). The patient pays 20% coinsurance — typically $10-$25 per session. Medicare uses a therapy spending threshold (approximately $2,330 for PT and SLP combined in 2026) above which claims require a KX modifier and may be subject to targeted medical review. The Bottom Line

Physical therapy costs are manageable with insurance, unpredictable with high-deductible plans, and negotiable without insurance. The smartest move is to ask your PT office for a cost estimate based on your specific insurance before starting treatment. Know your deductible status, understand whether you'll pay a copay or coinsurance, and ask about cash-pay alternatives if the math works better for your situation.
