AI Scribes, the 8-Minute Rule, and PT Upcoding: What the BCBS Findings Mean for Outpatient Therapy

In March 2026, Blue Cross Blue Shield published the first published evidence of AI-driven upcoding at U.S. hospitals — $2.3 billion in excess inpatient and outpatient spending over three years, concentrated at the top 10% of hospitals by coding intensity growth. Outpatient physical therapy clinics weren't in the BCBS sample, but the same AI ambient-scribe and documentation tools driving the hospital pattern are now standard in PT practices. The audit consequences are already measurable.

Why PT Is Uniquely Exposed

Physical therapy is billed in timed units under the 8-minute rule. Each unit represents 8-22 minutes of one-on-one service. Small shifts in documented time change the billed unit count dramatically — 22 minutes of therapeutic exercise is one unit, 23 minutes is two. When an AI scribe generates a session note that rounds up "approximately 25 minutes" of therex into a documented 25 minutes, the billed unit goes from one to two without any clinical change.

Stack this across multiple CPT codes — 97110, 97112, 97140, 97530, 97535 — and a typical 45-minute outpatient visit can produce billing that ranges from 3 units to 5 depending on how time is documented. Payer AI catches the pattern at the clinician level within a quarter.

Where AI Scribes Drift in PT

  • 97110 therapeutic exercise unit inflation. The highest-volume PT code. AI notes tend to generalize time across interventions rather than documenting specific start-stop times per exercise.
  • 97140 manual therapy. Requires hands-on time that is documented, with the specific region treated. AI scribes that default to "manual therapy techniques performed" without a region and without a specific time attribution fail on medical review.
  • 97530 therapeutic activities. The distinction between 97530 (dynamic activities) and 97110 (specific exercises) is blurry to most AI scribes. When the AI consistently codes 97530 because it reimburses slightly higher in some fee schedules, the pattern is visible to the payer within a quarter.
  • Modifier 59 / X-modifier overuse. AI notes sometimes append modifier 59 to bypass NCCI edits without clinical justification. CMS has been tightening the NCCI edits for PT/OT specifically since 2023.
  • GP therapy modifier attestation. Required on every PT claim. Not inflated by AI but worth checking — some scribe-driven billing workflows drop it entirely on certain claim types.

What Payers Are Already Doing

Medicare outpatient therapy cap enforcement has been replaced by the targeted medical review threshold — currently $3,000 per year per beneficiary for PT/SLP combined. Above that threshold, your claims are more likely to be reviewed. AI-scribe-driven unit inflation makes beneficiaries cross the threshold faster, which puts the downstream claims in front of a reviewer.

Commercial payers, especially UnitedHealthcare and Aetna, are using their own AI claim-review systems to flag outlier providers. An independent PT clinic with a unit-per-visit average 20% above the regional median is now a routine audit target.

A Five-Chart Self-Audit You Can Run This Week

  1. Pull five recent visits with 4+ billed units.
  2. For each, verify the total billed time matches the documented start-stop time in the note.
  3. Verify every 97140 claim has a specific body region and a distinct time block.
  4. Check every modifier 59 for a documented clinical justification separate from the NCCI-pair codes.
  5. Confirm the GP modifier is on every PT line.

The Bottom Line

The 8-minute rule wasn't designed for AI scribes. AI scribes weren't designed for the 8-minute rule. When the two meet, the clinic that hasn't built an explicit time-verification step into its billing workflow is the one that ends up in front of a payer reviewer. The BCBS findings are the prologue — the next chapter is written in the outpatient therapy data CMS is already collecting.
