CPT Code 97110: Therapeutic Exercise Billing Guide

By ClearPhysicalTherapyRates · April 2026 · 9 min read

CPT 97110 — Therapeutic Exercise — is the single most frequently billed code in physical therapy. It covers exercises designed to develop strength, endurance, flexibility, and range of motion. Because of its high utilization, it is also one of the most commonly audited codes. Understanding exactly what 97110 covers, how to bill it correctly, and what documentation is required protects both your revenue and your compliance standing.

What CPT 97110 Covers

97110 is defined as therapeutic procedures to develop strength, endurance, flexibility, and range of motion. Each unit represents 15 minutes of direct one-on-one time with the patient. The therapist must be present and actively involved — setting up a patient on equipment and walking away is not billable under 97110.

Common activities billed under 97110 include progressive resistive exercises for strengthening, active and active-assisted range of motion exercises, stretching programs for flexibility, endurance training (stationary bike, treadmill walking with therapist guidance), core stabilization exercises, and aerobic conditioning within therapeutic context.

97110 vs. Other Therapeutic Codes

The distinction between 97110 and related codes is a frequent source of billing errors:

  • 97110 vs. 97530 (Therapeutic Activities) — 97110 focuses on isolated exercise parameters (strength, ROM, endurance). 97530 involves dynamic, functional activities that simulate real-life tasks (practicing sit-to-stand, carrying objects, climbing stairs). If the patient is doing bicep curls, that's 97110. If they're practicing getting groceries off a shelf, that's 97530.
  • 97110 vs. 97140 (Manual Therapy) — 97110 is exercise-based; the patient is actively performing movements. 97140 is hands-on treatment by the therapist — joint mobilization, soft tissue mobilization, manual traction. The patient is relatively passive during 97140.
  • 97110 vs. 97542 (Wheelchair Management) — Wheelchair mobility training uses 97542, not 97110, even though it involves exercise-like activities.

Audit flag: Billing 97110 and 97530 for the same time period is a common denial trigger. If you're billing both codes in the same session, the documentation must clearly show different activities during separate, non-overlapping time periods. An auditor reading the note should be able to identify which minutes were 97110 and which were 97530.

Time-Based Billing Rules

97110 is a timed code billed in 15-minute units. The 8-minute rule applies: you must provide at least 8 minutes of the service to bill one unit. For multiple timed codes in the same session, CMS uses the following standard:

  • 8-22 minutes total timed services = 1 unit
  • 23-37 minutes = 2 units
  • 38-52 minutes = 3 units
  • 53-67 minutes = 4 units

When billing multiple timed codes in one session, add up all timed minutes, determine the total number of billable units, then allocate units to the codes that received the most minutes. This is called the "rule of eights" allocation method.

Documentation Requirements

Every 97110 claim must be supported by documentation that includes the specific exercises performed (not just "therapeutic exercise"), the body region and condition being treated, the number of sets, repetitions, resistance level, or duration, the patient's response and performance, how the exercise relates to functional goals in the plan of care, and the exact time spent on 97110 activities (start/stop or total minutes).

A note that says "patient performed therapeutic exercises for 30 minutes" is insufficient. A compliant note says "Patient performed seated hamstring curls 3x10 at 5 lbs, standing hip abduction 3x10 with yellow band, and supine bridges 3x12 for bilateral glute strengthening — 32 minutes total. Patient tolerated well, demonstrated improved form compared to last session."

Medicare-Specific Considerations

For Medicare patients, 97110 is subject to the therapy cap (now a threshold rather than a hard cap). Once a patient's therapy spending exceeds the annual threshold amount, claims require a KX modifier certifying medical necessity and are subject to targeted medical review. Documentation must be particularly strong for patients approaching or exceeding the threshold.

Medicare also requires that therapeutic exercise be "skilled" — meaning it requires the expertise of a therapist to design, monitor, or modify. If a patient can safely perform the exercises independently without therapist guidance, the service may not meet the skilled care standard.

Common Billing Errors

  • Billing 97110 for group exercise — 97110 requires direct one-on-one contact. Group therapy uses 97150.
  • Billing 97110 for unsupervised exercise — If the patient exercises independently while you treat another patient, that time is not billable under 97110.
  • Overlapping 97110 with other timed codes — You cannot bill 97110 and another timed code for the same minutes.
  • Under-documenting exercise specifics — Generic descriptions without sets, reps, resistance, and body region will not survive audit.
  • Billing 97110 for activities better described by 97530 — Functional activities should be coded as 97530, not forced into 97110.

The Bottom Line

97110 is the foundation of physical therapy billing — and getting it right means documenting specific exercises with measurable parameters, tracking time accurately, distinguishing it from related codes, and ensuring every session connects to functional goals in the plan of care. The few extra minutes spent on thorough documentation pay for themselves many times over in clean claims and audit protection.
